    Section 2.1 DE1: Average/Instantaneous Rates of Change

    Objectives

    DE1
    I can use a limit definition to compute derivatives at a point, explain how average rates of change are used in the process, and interpret the meaning of the derivative in context.

    Definition 2.1.1. Average Rate of Change.

    Let \(f(x)\) be a continuous function on some interval \([a,b]\text{.}\) For two inputs \(x_1\) and \(x_2\) in \([a,b]\text{,}\) the average rate of change of \(f(x)\) between \(x_1\) and \(x_2\) is given by
    \begin{equation*} \dfrac{f(x_2)-f(x_1)}{x_2-x_1} \end{equation*}
    In the context of motion, the average rate of change of position is called average velocity.

    Activity 2.1.3.

    In this activity you will study the velocity of a ball falling under gravity. The height of the ball (in feet) is given by the formula \(f(t) = 64-16(t-1)^2\text{,}\) where \(t\) is measured in seconds. For your convenience, below you will find a table of values for \(f(t)\text{.}\)
    (a)

    (b)

    (c)
    What was the average velocity on the interval \([1.75,2]\text{?}\) What about on the interval \([2,2.25]\text{?}\)
    (d)

    Definition 2.1.3. Instantaneous Rate of Change.

    The instantaneous rate of change of a function \(f\) at the input \(x=a\)is given by
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{h\to 0} \dfrac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h} \end{equation*}

    Activity 2.1.5.

    There is a way to make the definition of instantaneous rate of change match with the expression given in Task (b) of Activity 2.1.4. What is the connection between the two expressions?

    Definition 2.1.4. Secant Line.

    Let \(f(x)\) be a continuous function on \([a,b]\text{,}\) and let \(x_1\) and \(x_2\) be inputs in \([a,b]\text{.}\) Then, the secant line between \(x_1\) and \(x_2\) is the line which goes through the points \((x_1,f(x_1))\) and \((x_2,f(x_2))\)

    Definition 2.1.5. Tangent Line.

    Let \(f(x)\) be a continuous function in a neighborhood of the input \(x=a\text{.}\) The tangent line to \(f\) and \(a\) is the line which only intercepts \(f(x)\) “intentionally” at \(x=a\text{.}\)

    Is it possible to find the instantaneous rate of change of \(f(x) = 3-2x\) at \(x=\pi\) without doing any computations?
    1. No, because we cannot compute the value \(f(\pi)\text{.}\)
    2. No, because we cannot compute the average rate of change on the interval \([\pi, \pi+h]\text{.}\)
    3. Yes, because the intercept of the tangent line at any point is equal to the constant intercept of \(f(x)\text{.}\)
    4. Yes, because the slope of the tangent line at any point is equal to the constant slope of \(f(x)\text{.}\)

    Definition 2.1.6. Derivative (at a point).

    The derivative of \(f(x)\) at the input \(x=a\) is given by the expression
    \begin{equation*} f'(a) = \lim_{x\to a} \dfrac{f(x) - f(a)}{x-a} \end{equation*}
    or
    \begin{equation*} f'(a) = \lim_{h\to 0} \dfrac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h} \end{equation*}
    assuming this limit exists.

    Remark 2.1.7.

    The derivative of a function \(f(x)\) at the input \(x=a\) can be written as
    \begin{equation*} f'(a)\qquad \dfrac{df}{dx}\bigg\rvert_{x=a} \qquad \dot{f}(a) \end{equation*}
    The first notation is called prime notation, the second is called Leibniz notation, and the third is called flyspeck notation.

    Remark 2.1.8.

    When talking about an instantaneous rate of change, present progessive verbs should be used (-ing words). This is because the change is happening at this moment in time, as opposed to an averate rate of change, which occurs over a period of time.

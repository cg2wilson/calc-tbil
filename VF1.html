    Section 12.1 VF1: Parametrizing Curves

    Objectives

    VF1
    I can parametrize curves and use calculus to analyze their behavior.

    Pre-Class Preparation.

    Read the following sections of the text:
    • Section 1.1: Parametric Equations
      Read all sections
    • Section 1.2: Calculus of Parametric Curves
      Read all sections

    Subsection 12.1.1 In-Class Activities

    Definition 12.1.1. Parametric Curve.

    If \(x\) and \(y\) are continuous functions of the variable \(t\) on some interval \(I\text{,}\) then the equations \(x = x(t)\) and \(y = y(t)\) are called parametric equations and \(t\) is called the parameter. The graph of parametric equations- the set of points \((x,y)\) obtained as \(t\) varies over \(I\)- is called a parametric curve.

    Remark 12.1.2.

    Calculus on parametric curves can be done using \(t\) as the independent variable and without eliminating the parameter!

    Theorem 12.1.3. Derivatives of Parametric Equations.

    For the plane curve defined by the parametric equations \(x = x(t)\) and \(y = y(t)\) on the interval \(I\text{,}\) the derivative \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\) is given by
    \begin{equation*} \dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{dy/dt}{dx/dt} = \dfrac{y'(t)}{x'(t)} \end{equation*}
    assuming that \(x'(t),y'(t)\) exist and \(x'(t)\neq 0\text{.}\)
    The second derivative, \(\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}\text{,}\) is given by
    \begin{equation*} \dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2} = \dfrac{\frac{d}{dt}[dy/dx]}{x'(t)} \end{equation*}
    under the same assumptions.

    Activity 12.1.8.

    Compute the derivative \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\) for the plane curve defined by the equations \(x(t) = t^2-4t\text{,}\) \(y(t) = 2t^3-6t\text{,}\) on \(-2\leq t\leq 3\text{.}\) Identify any critical points of the function.
    Answer.
    \(\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{3t^2-1}{t-2}\text{.}\) The critical points occur at \(t = \pm \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{3}}\) and \(t=2\)

    Activity 12.1.9.

    Consider the parameterization of a circle given by \(x(t) = a\cos t\text{,}\) \(y = a\sin t\)
    (a)
    Find the derivative at any arbitrary point \((x_0,y_0)\) on the curve. Does the derivative depend on the size of the circle?
    Answer.
    \(\dfrac{dy}{dx} = -\cot (t)\text{.}\) No, it does not.
    (b)
    Find the second derivative at any arbitrary point. Does the acceleration depend on the size of the circle?
    Answer.
    \(\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2} = \dfrac{1}{a}\csc^3(t)\text{.}\) Yes, it does.

    Theorem 12.1.4. Area Under a Parametric Curve.

    Let \(x=x(t)\) and \(y = y(t)\) be a non-self-intersecting plane curve on the interval \(a\leq t\leq b\text{;}\) assume that \(x(t)\) is differentiable. Then, the area under the curve is given by
    \begin{equation*} A = \int_a^b y(t) x'(t)\, dt \end{equation*}

    Activity 12.1.10.

    A cycloid is a figure traced out by the edge of a rolling circle. The parametric equations for a cycloid of radius \(r\) are given by \(x(\theta) = r(\theta - \sin\theta)\) and \(y(\theta) = r(1-\cos\theta)\text{.}\)
    (a)
    Compute the derivative for a cycloid. Does the derivative depend on the size of the circle?
    Answer.
    \(\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{1-\cos\theta}{\sin\theta}\)
    (b)
    Find the area under one arch of the cycloid
    Answer.
    \(A = 3\pi r^2\)

    Theorem 12.1.5. Arc Length of a Parametric Curve.

    Consider the plane curve defined by the parametric equations \(x = x(t)\text{,}\) \(y = y(t)\) on the interval \(t_1 \leq t\leq t_2\text{,}\) and assume that both functions are differentiable. Then, the arc length of the curve is given by
    \begin{equation*} s = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \sqrt{\lrpar{\dfrac{dx}{dt}}^2 + \lrpar{\dfrac{dy}{dt}}^2} \end{equation*}

    Activity 12.1.11.

    Use Theorem 12.1.5 to prove that the circumference of a circle is given by \(2\pi r\)
    Answer.
    Consider the parametrization \(x(t) = r\cos t\text{,}\) \(y(t) = r\sin t\) on \(0\leq t\leq 2\pi\text{.}\) Then, \(x'(t) = -r\sin t\) and \(y'(t) = r\cos t\) so that
    \begin{equation*} \sqrt{(x')^2 + (y')^2} = \sqrt{(-r\sin t)^2 + (r\cos t)^2} = r \end{equation*}
    Then, arc length is given by
    \begin{equation*} s = \int_0^{2\pi} r\, dt = 2\pi r \end{equation*}

    Activity 12.1.12.

    Find the arc length of the curve defined by the equations \(x(t) = 3t^2\text{,}\) \(y(t) = 2t^3\text{,}\) \(1\leq t\leq 3\text{.}\)
    Answer.
    \(4\sqrt{2}(5\sqrt{5} -1)\)

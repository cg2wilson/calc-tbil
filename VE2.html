    Section 5.2 VE2: Dot Products and Cross Products

    Objectives

    VE2
    I can compute and apply the dot product and cross product of vectors.

    Pre-Class Preparation.

    Read the following sections of the text:
    • Volume 3, Section 2.3: The Dot Product
      Read all sections
    • Volume 3, Section 2.4: The Cross Product
      Read all sections

    Subsection 5.2.1 In-Class Activities

    Remark 5.2.1.

    In Section 5.1, we learned how to add vectors and multiply vectors with scalar values; we didn’t talk about multiplying two vectors.
    There are two ways of multiplying vectors: the dot product and the cross product.

    Definition 5.2.2. Dot Product (Algebraic Definition).

    Given two vectors \(\textbf{a} = \lrangle{a_1,a_2,a_3}\) and \(\textbf{b} = \lrangle{b_1,b_2,b_3}\text{,}\) the dot product of \(\textbf{a}\) and \(\textbf{b}\) is given by
    \begin{equation*} \textbf{a}\cdot \textbf{b} = a_1b_1 + a_2b_2+a_3b_3 \end{equation*}
    The dot product is sometimes also called the scalar product because it results in a scalar value rather than a vector value.

    Activity 5.2.1.

    Which option gives the dot product \(\lrangle{10,-1,2}\cdot\lrangle{-8,4,0}\text{?}\)
    1. \(\displaystyle \lrangle{2,3,2}\)
    2. 7
    3. \(\displaystyle \lrangle{-80,-4,0}\)
    4. \(\displaystyle -84\)

    Activity 5.2.2.

    This activity will introduce some properties of the dot product. Let \(\textbf{u} = \lrangle{1,0,2}\text{,}\) \(\textbf{v} = \lrangle{-1,3,4}\text{,}\) and \(\textbf{w} = \lrangle{0,-2,-1}\)
    (a)
    Find \(||\textbf{u}||\text{,}\) \(||\textbf{v}||\text{,}\) and \(||\textbf{w}||\text{.}\)
    (b)
    Compute \(\textbf{u}\cdot\textbf{v}\)
    (c)
    Which of these statements is true about \(\textbf{v}\cdot \textbf{u}\text{?}\) Justify your response.
    1. It is similar but not the same as \(\textbf{u}\cdot \textbf{v}\)
    2. It is the same as \(\textbf{u}\cdot \textbf{v}\)
    3. It is not similar to \(\textbf{u}\cdot \textbf{v}\)
    (d)
    Compute \(\textbf{v}+\textbf{w}\) and \(\textbf{u}\cdot\textbf{w}\text{.}\)
    (e)
    True or false: \(\textbf{u}\cdot (\textbf{v}+\textbf{w}) = \textbf{u}\cdot \textbf{v} + \textbf{w}\text{.}\) Justify your answer.
    1. True
    2. False
    (f)
    Let \(c\) be a scalar value. For the value \(c(\textbf{u}\cdot \textbf{v})\text{,}\) does it matter if we compute \((c\textbf{u})\cdot \textbf{v}\) or \(\textbf{u}\cdot (c\textbf{v})\text{?}\) Why or why not?
    1. Yes, it matters because the values are different from \(c(\textbf{u}\cdot \textbf{v})\)
    2. Yes, because we get a vector value when we should get a scalar value
    3. No, because we get a scalar value when we expect a scalar value
    4. No, because the values are the same as \(c(\textbf{u}\cdot \textbf{v})\)
    (g)
    Compute \(\textbf{u}\cdot \textbf{u}\) and \(\textbf{v}\cdot \textbf{v}\text{.}\) How do these compare with the lengths you computed in part (a)?
    (h)
    Use the previous part to predict the value of \(\textbf{w}\cdot\textbf{w}\)

    Properties of the Dot Product.

    Let \(\textbf{u},\textbf{v}\text{,}\) and \(\textbf{w}\) be vectors, and let \(c\) be a scalar value.
    1. (Commutative Property)
      \begin{equation*} \textbf{u}\cdot\textbf{v} = \textbf{v}\cdot\textbf{u} \end{equation*}
    2. (Distributive Property)
      \begin{equation*} \textbf{u}\cdot (\textbf{v}+\textbf{w}) = \textbf{u}\cdot\textbf{v} + \textbf{u}\cdot\textbf{w} \end{equation*}
    3. (Associative Property)
      \begin{equation*} c(\textbf{u}\cdot\textbf{v}) = (c\textbf{u})\cdot\textbf{v} = \textbf{u}\cdot (c\textbf{v}) \end{equation*}
    4. (Property of magnitude)
      \begin{equation*} \textbf{u}\cdot \textbf{u} = ||\textbf{u}||^2 \end{equation*}

    Definition 5.2.3. Dot Product (Geometric Definition).

    If the angle between the two vectors \(\textbf{u}\) and \(\textbf{v}\) is given by \(\theta\) (in radians), then
    \begin{equation*} \textbf{u}\cdot\textbf{v} = ||\textbf{u}||\,||\textbf{v}||\cos\theta \end{equation*}

    Activity 5.2.3.

    Which is the correct angle between the vectors \(\textbf{i}+\textbf{j}-\textbf{k}\) and \(\textbf{i} -2\textbf{j}-3\textbf{k}\text{?}\)
    1. \(\displaystyle \)
    2. \(\displaystyle \)
    3. \(\displaystyle \)
    4. \(\displaystyle \)

    Activity 5.2.4.

    Two vectors are said to be orthogonal if the angle between them is \(\dfrac{\pi}{2}\) or \(90\dc\text{.}\)
    (a)
    What is the value of the dot product for two orthogonal vectors?
    1. \(\displaystyle 1\)
    2. \(\displaystyle -1\)
    3. Any value
    4. 0
    (b)
    Are the vectors \(\lrangle{10,0,5}\) and \(\lrangle{1,3,-2}\) orthogonal? Justify your answer.
    (c)
    For what value or values of \(x\) is the vector \(\textbf{p} = \lrangle{2,8,-1}\) orthogonal to \(\textbf{q} = \lrangle{x,-1,2}\text{?}\)

    Activity 5.2.5.

    When constant force is applied to an object so that the object moves in a straight line from point \(P\) to point \(Q\text{,}\) the work \(W\) done by the form \(\textbf{F}\text{,}\) acting at an angle \(\theta\) from the line of motion, is given by
    \begin{equation*} W = \textbf{F}\cdot \textbf{PQ} = ||\textbf{F}||\, ||\textbf{PQ}||\cos\theta \end{equation*}
    A constant force of 30 pounds is applied at angle of \(60\dc\) to pull a handcart 10 feet across the ground. What is the work done by this force?

    Definition 5.2.4. Cross Product (Algebraic Definition).

    The cross product, or vector product, of two vectors \(\textbf{a} = \lrangle{a_1,a_2,a_3}\) and \(\textbf{b} = \lrangle{b_1,b_2,b_3}\) is given by
    \begin{equation*} \textbf{a}\times\textbf{b} = \lrangle{a_2b_3-a_3b_2,a_3b_1-a_1b_3,a_1b_2-a_2b_1} \end{equation*}

    Remark 5.2.5.

    The cross product of two vectors \(\textbf{a}\) and \(\textbf{b}\) produces a third vector, \(\textbf{a}\times\textbf{b}\text{,}\) which is orthogonal to \(\textbf{a}\) and \(\textbf{b}\) according to the right-hand rule.

    Activity 5.2.6.

    Let \(\textbf{a} = \lrangle{-1,2,3}\) and \(\textbf{b} = \lrangle{1,0,-1}\text{.}\)
    (a)
    Which vector gives the cross product \(\textbf{a}\times\textbf{b}\text{?}\)
    1. \(\displaystyle \lrangle{-2,2,-2}\)
    2. \(\displaystyle \lrangle{2,-2,2}\)
    3. \(\displaystyle \lrangle{2,2,2}\)
    4. \(\displaystyle \lrangle{-2,-2,-2}\)
    (b)
    Which option gives the cross product \(\textbf{b}\times\textbf{a}\text{?}\)
    1. \(\displaystyle \lrangle{-2,2,-2}\)
    2. \(\displaystyle \lrangle{2,-2,2}\)
    3. \(\displaystyle \lrangle{2,2,2}\)
    4. \(\displaystyle \lrangle{-2,-2,-2}\)
    (c)
    In order to be commutative, it must be true that \(\textbf{a}\times\textbf{b} = \textbf{b}\times\textbf{a}\text{.}\) Is the cross product commutative? What relationship, if any, can you establish from this task?

    Properties of the Cross Product.

    Let \(\textbf{u},\textbf{v},\textbf{w}\) be three-dimensional vectors, and let \(c\) be a scalar. Then, the following properties hold:
    1. (Anticommutative Property) \(\textbf{u}\times\textbf{v} = -\textbf{v}\times\textbf{u}\)
    2. (Distributive Property) \(\textbf{u}\times (\textbf{v}+\textbf{w}) = \textbf{u}\times\textbf{v} + \textbf{u}\times\textbf{w}\)
    3. (Constant Multiple Property) \(c(\textbf{u}\times\textbf{v}) = (c\textbf{u})\times \textbf{v} = \textbf{u}\times (c\textbf{v})\)
    4. (Cross Product of Zero Vector) \(\textbf{u}\times\textbf{0} = \textbf{0}\times\textbf{u} = \textbf{0}\)
    5. (Cross Product of a Vector with Itself) \(\textbf{u}\times\textbf{u} = \textbf{0}\)

    Activity 5.2.7.

    Use the properties of the cross product to compute \((3\textbf{j}\times 2\textbf{k})\times (-\textbf{i})\)

    Definition 5.2.6. Cross Product (Geometric Definition).

    Let \(\textbf{u}\) and \(\textbf{v}\) be two vectors, and let \(\theta\) be the angle between them. Then,
    \begin{equation*} ||\textbf{u}\times\textbf{v}|| = ||\textbf{u}||\,||\textbf{v}||\sin\theta \end{equation*}

    Activity 5.2.8.

    Find a unit vector orthogonal to both \(\textbf{u} = \lrangle{4,0,3}\) and \(\textbf{w} = \lrangle{1,1,4}\)

    Activity 5.2.9.

    Torque is the tendency of a force to produce rotation about an axis of rotation. Let \(\textbf{r}\) is a vector whose initial point is located on the axis of rotation and whose terminal point is located at the point where the force is applied. Let \(\textbf{F}\) be the vector representing the force. Then,
    \begin{equation*} \mathbf{\tau} = \textbf{r}\times\textbf{F} \end{equation*}
    where \(\mathbf{\tau}\) represents the torque vector.
    A bolt is tightened by applying a 10 N force to a wrench of length 0.3 meters; the angle between the wrench and the force vector is \(40\dc\text{.}\) Find the magnitude of the torque about the center of the bolt.

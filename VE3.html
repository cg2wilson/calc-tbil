    Section 5.3 VE3: Lines and Planes in Space

    Objectives

    VE3
    I can determine information about lines and planes in space, including: distance, angles between objects, and equations in various forms.

    Pre-Class Preparation.

    Read the following sections of the text:
    • Volume 3, Section 2.5: Equations of Lines and Planes in Space
      Read all sections

    Subsection 5.3.1 In-Class Activities

    Remark 5.3.1.

    In three dimensions, lines can take slightly different form than we are accustomed to in two dimensions.

    Definition 5.3.2. Direction Vector.

    The direction vector of a line is a vector which points in the same direction as the line.

    Fact 5.3.3.

    Two vectors are parallel if they have the same or opposite directions. This means that there is some scalar value \(k\) such that
    \begin{equation*} \textbf{u} = k\textbf{v} \end{equation*}
    or equivalently that, if the vectors have the same direction
    \begin{equation*} k = \dfrac{||\textbf{u}||}{||\textbf{v}||} \end{equation*}
    or
    \begin{equation*} k = -\dfrac{||\textbf{u}||}{||\textbf{v}||} \end{equation*}
    if they have opposite directions.

    Activity 5.3.1.

    This activity will develop the vector equation of a line.
    Let \(L\) be a line in space passing through the point \(P(x_0,y_0,z_0)\) and let \(\textbf{v} = \lrangle{a,b,c}\) be a vector parallel to \(L\text{.}\)
    (a)
    Let \(Q(x,y,z)\) be another point on \(L\text{.}\) Write the vector \(\textbf{PQ}\text{.}\)
    (b)
    Since \(Q\) is on \(L\text{,}\) \(\textbf{v}\) will also be parallel to \(\textbf{PQ}\text{.}\) Using Fact 5.3.3, write an expression (using \(t\) for your scalar value) relating \(\textbf{PQ}\) and \(\textbf{v}\text{.}\)
    (c)
    Use vector operations to complete the equation: \(\lrangle{x,y,z} = \)

    Parametric/Symmetric Forms of Lines.

    Let the line \(L\) go through the points \((x_0,y_0,z_0)\) and have direction vector \(\lrangle{a,b,c}\text{.}\)
    Then, \(L\) can be expressed in parametric form as
    \begin{equation*} x = x_0+ta\qquad y = y_0+tb\qquad z = z_0+tc \end{equation*}
    and in symmetric form as
    \begin{equation*} t = \dfrac{x-x_0}{a} = \dfrac{y-y_0}{b} = \dfrac{z-z_0}{c} \end{equation*}

    Activity 5.3.2.

    Let \(L\) be the line passing through the points \((1,4,-2)\) and \((0,-3,5)\text{.}\)
    (a)
    Which expression gives the parametric form of the line?
    1. \begin{align*} x \amp = 1 -t \\ y \amp = 4 -7t\\ z \amp = -2+7t \end{align*}
    2. \begin{align*} x \amp = 1 -t \\ y \amp = 4 -7t\\ z \amp = -2+7t \end{align*}
    (b)
    Use part (a) to write the symmetric form of the line.

    Activity 5.3.3.

    To find the distance between a point and a line, recall that the cross product of two vectors \(\textbf{a}\) and \(\textbf{b}\) produces a third vector \(\textbf{a}\times\textbf{b}\) which is orthogonal to both \(\textbf{a}\) and \(\textbf{b}\text{.}\)
    Let \(L\) be a line in space passing through the point \(P\) with direction vector \(\textbf{v}\text{.}\) Let \(M\) be a point which is not on \(L\text{.}\)
    (a)
    First, create a sketch with \(L\text{,}\) \(P\text{,}\) \(\textbf{v}\text{,}\) and \(M\text{.}\)
    (b)
    Create a parallelogram whose sides are the vector \(\textbf{PM}\) and \(\textbf{v}\text{.}\) It is a fact that the area of the parallelogram is given by \(||\textbf{PM}\times\textbf{v}||\text{.}\)
    Write another expression which gives the area of the parallelogram, using \(d\) for the distance between \(M\) and \(L\text{.}\)
    (c)
    Solve for \(d\) to get the formula for the distance from a point to a line in space.

    Distance from a Point to a Line.

    Consider line \(L\) in space which passes through \(P\) with direction vector \(\textbf{v}\text{.}\) If \(M\) is a point not on \(L\text{,}\) then the distance from \(M\) to \(L\) is given by
    \begin{equation*} d = \dfrac{||\textbf{PM}\times\textbf{v}||}{||\textbf{v}||} \end{equation*}

    Activity 5.3.4.

    Find the distance between \(M = (0,3,-2)\) and the line given by
    \begin{equation*} \dfrac{x-3}{4}=\dfrac{y+1}{2} = z-3 \end{equation*}
    (a)
    Which is the correct direction vector for the line?
    1. \(\displaystyle \lrangle{-3,1,-3}\)
    2. \(\displaystyle \lrangle{3,-1,3}\)
    3. \(\displaystyle \lrangle{4,2,1}\)
    4. \(\displaystyle \lrangle{1,1,1}\)
    (b)
    Why do we know the point \(P(3,-1,3)\) lies on the line?
    (c)
    \(\textbf{PM}\) is given by which of the following?
    1. \(\displaystyle \lrangle{3,-4,5}\)
    2. \(\displaystyle \lrangle{0,0,0}\)
    3. \(\displaystyle \lrangle{-3,4,-5}\)
    4. \(\displaystyle \lrangle{6,-2,6}\)
    (d)
    Compute the distance between \(M\) and the line.

    Definition 5.3.4. Skew Lines.

    Two lines are called skew when they are neither parallel nor intersecting.

    Activity 5.3.5.

    Consider the lines given by the parametric equations
    \begin{equation*} x = 1-4t\qquad y = 3+t\qquad z=8-6t \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} x = 2+3s\qquad y = 2s\qquad z = -1-3s \end{equation*}
    True or false: the lines are skew
    1. True
    2. False

    Definition 5.3.5. Normal Vector.

    A normal vector to a surface is one which is orthogonal (or perpendicular) to that surface.

    Activity 5.3.6.

    Let the points \(P(x_0,y_0,z_0)\) and \(Q(x,y,z)\) be points in a plane.
    (a)
    Let \(\textbf{n}\) be a vector normal to the plane at \(P\text{.}\) Which expression mathematically describes this relationship?
    1. \(\displaystyle \textbf{n}\cdot \textbf{QP} = 1\)
    2. \(\displaystyle \textbf{n}\cdot \textbf{PQ} = 0\)
    3. \(\displaystyle \textbf{n}\times\textbf{PQ} = 0\)
    4. \(\displaystyle \textbf{n}\times\textbf{QP} = 0\)
    (b)
    If \(\textbf{n} = \lrangle{a,b,c}\text{,}\) use your answer in part (a) to rewrite without vector operations

    Definition 5.3.6. Scalar Equation of a Plane.

    The scalar equation of a plane containing the point \(P(x_0,y_0,z_0)\) with normal vector \(\textbf{n} = \lrangle{a,b,c}\) is given by
    \begin{equation*} a(x-x_0) + b(y-y_0) + c(z-z_0) = 0 \end{equation*}
    or alternately,
    \begin{equation*} ax + by + cz + d = 0 \end{equation*}

    Activity 5.3.7.

    We will find the equation of the plane containing points \(P(1,1,1)\text{,}\) \(Q(2,0,1)\text{,}\) and \(R(-1,-1,0)\)
    (a)
    First we need to find a normal vector for the plane. Which operation will find a vector orthogonal to a vector on the plane?
    1. Dot product
    2. Cross product
    (b)
    Find the vectors \(\textbf{PQ}\) and \(\textbf{PR}\text{,}\) then use the operation from part (a) to find the orthogonal vector.
    (c)
    Use any point and your answer to part (b) to find the equation of the plane.
